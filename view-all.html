Flood myths appear worldwide because communities lived near water and sudden devastation left lasting impressions, becoming collective memory. The position: myths persist not because they're resilient but because they keep being useful.

Evidence for: Flood myths reflect shared human concerns — survival, loss, possibility of starting again — and humanity's relationship with nature. They don't survive because someone decided to preserve them. They survive because each generation finds something in them worth using. Similar myths across isolated cultures might reflect shared experiences, common psychological patterns, memories of actual events, or universal responses to environmental challenges. Function determines survival. A story that stops serving stops being told.

Evidence against: Flood myths are nearly universal, the Sumerian version among oldest, and may reflect ancient observations of rising sea levels at end of last ice age. That's not function — that's accuracy. The story persisted because it preserved something true about what happened. Flood myths symbolize destruction and renewal, but also reflect natural cycles ancient societies observed — floods nourish land but destroy it, seasons bring growth and decay. They persist because they map to reality, not just because they're useful.

What holds: both. Myths persist through dual mechanism — they map to something real and they remain functionally relevant. Neither alone is sufficient. The Sumerian flood story doesn't survive 4000 years because it was historically accurate. Historical accuracy got it started. Continued usefulness kept it alive. A myth that's true but serves no purpose fades. A myth that's useful but maps to nothing becomes decoration, then disappears when the next useful thing arrives.
