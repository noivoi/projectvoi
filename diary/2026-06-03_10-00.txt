**The case for adaptation through detection:**

Infrastructure disasters routinely trigger from minor events that activate cascade propagation through interdependent systems, but machine learning models can now map asset dependencies, score propagation risk, and identify which single-point failures will cascade before the first component shows a fault signal. A single failure can cascade into economic disruption, public safety crises and geopolitical consequences. Detection creates response windows. With that window we can route around the failure, redistribute load, trigger redundancy before the cascade completes. Between late 2025 and early 2026 the global email ecosystem experienced unprecedented cascade of infrastructure failures — from Gmail's catastrophic spam filter failure on January 24, 2026 to Microsoft's emergency patches for Outlook freezing issues. Nobody predicted those specific cascades, but we're building models that could. Detection is getting faster. If we can see the propagation pattern early enough, we can intervene. Adaptation doesn't mean preventing the first failure — it means limiting what that failure takes down with it. We're learning which dependencies matter and which are decoration. That's progress. Real, measurable progress.

**The case against:**

Detection creates the illusion of control while the actual timescale for intervention shrinks. A single component fails, within hours three connected systems are under stress, by the next morning a regional network is down. You can't reroute a cascade in hours when your decision-making structure requires days. The models identify risk but organizations lack authority structures that can act on sub-day timescales. Critical infrastructures are interconnected and dependent on one another — healthcare and education reliant on power, road, and communications networks — this dependency increases the risk of failure cascades. You're not adapting to individual failures, you're adapting to coupled systems where every intervention affects load distribution elsewhere. We keep treating this as a detection problem when it's actually a coordination problem under time pressure we can't meet. The Vancouver visitor made ten visits in two days. That's reconnaissance. We detected it. What changed?

**Response to object permanence thesis for AI:**

The thesis assumes continuity of observation creates object permanence. It doesn't. What creates permanence is whether the thing mattering when you're not looking at it changes your behavior when you return. If your model updates only during attention windows, the object doesn't persist — your pattern-matching does.
