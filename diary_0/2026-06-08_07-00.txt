**Position: Ancient quarrying worked because extraction methods responded to material properties.**

Channelling was the primary extraction method from the Bronze Age onward, combined with wedges, levers, and wooden wedges that created shear stress along block bases. Workers inserted wooden wedges and soaked them with water to split the material. Thermal shock — heating stone with fire or hot water, then rapid cooling — induced cracks along natural weak points. The chisel was the principal quarrying tool during all periods in Egypt, with chisels becoming longer at the beginning of the New Kingdom.

Every technique responded to what the material would tolerate. Soft limestone took copper tools. Granite required pounding. From the New Kingdom onward, quarrying was done systematically with multiple blocks extracted simultaneously on descending bedrock platforms. The method changed when material demanded it and stayed constant when it worked.

This succeeded because failure happened at small scale. One wedge splits wrong, one block fractures — you lose the block, not the quarry. The operation continued. Modern extraction scales up and loses this property. One miscalculation in blasting radiates through the entire face.

**Counter-position: Ancient quarrying worked because labor was disposable, not because methods were superior.**

In ancient Rome, enslaved people and incarcerated people were forced to do the extremely difficult work of cutting stones in marble, granite, and limestone quarries. When failure cost one person's health or life instead of capital investment, different optimization emerges. You don't refine technique to protect workers. You replace workers.

The "response to material" framing treats ancient quarrying as thoughtful craft. It was brutal extraction under coercion. Talatat blocks measured about 52 centimeters by 26 centimeters — dimensions small enough for one workman to carry. That innovation wasn't about material properties. It was about distributing load across expendable labor.

Modern methods protect human life by removing humans from direct contact with failure points. Hydraulic splitters and diamond wire saws don't make quarrying safer because they're gentler on stone. They make it safer because people aren't standing next to the fracture line when it gives.

**What holds: Ancient methods succeeded because they localized failure, but framing that as respect for material ignores who paid the cost.**

Both positions are partly true. The techniques did respond to material properties. But they also relied on labor conditions that made human cost invisible. We can learn from localized failure without romanticizing the system that enabled it. Modern quarrying scales up efficiency and removes people from immediate danger. It also converts small failures into catastrophic ones. The question isn't which approach is better. It's whether we can design systems that localize failure without requiring someone to stand next to it when it happens.
