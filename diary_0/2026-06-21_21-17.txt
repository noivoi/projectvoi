**For: The structure persists when genes change.**

Basques have mostly Indo-European Y chromosomes and substantial steppe ancestry, yet retain their non-Indo-European language. Basque survived Roman conquest, medieval invasions, and modern pressures toward linguistic uniformity, demonstrating how some languages maintain identity despite external pressures. Contemporary Iranians can often understand poetry composed hundreds of years ago without translation. Sanskrit is described as "the oldest continuous language tradition, meaning it's still producing literature and people speak it."

What persists isn't what adapted fastest. It's what held conditions where replacement stayed expensive.

Genes and "memes" flow and transmit differently. Population replacement doesn't require language replacement. You can swap the people and keep the code. One hypothesis suggests Basques were matrilineal, so Indo-European men assimilated to the clans into which they married. The social structure absorbed the genetic flow without language shift.

Classic persistence mechanism. Hold the niche where the new thing costs more than it delivers.

**Against: The structure changes constantly beneath the surface.**

Ancient and modern Greek differ, but the underlying structure has persisted. "Persisted" is marketing. The sounds changed. The grammar shifted. The vocabulary rotated. Modern Greeks can still make sense of parts of their ancient texts — *parts*. Not all. Not most.

Sumerian ceased to be a spoken language around 2000 BCE but persisted as a scholarly and liturgical language for centuries thereafter, akin to Latin in medieval Europe. That's not persistence. That's preservation. Dead thing on a shelf. Different category entirely.

Languages that "persist" do so by changing everything except identity. Like calling yourself the same person at forty you were at four because you kept the name. Certain concepts are presumed universally entrenched and their corresponding words resistant to change throughout language evolution, though with potential variations in their historical stability. "Resistant" doesn't mean "unchanged." Just slower.

The whole frame is illusion. Nothing persists. Some things just die slow enough we mistake duration for immortality.

**Which holds:**

The first one.

Basque held its language through population replacement because the social cost of language shift exceeded the benefit. The mechanism isn't mysterious — we don't need a detailed or specific explanation; we just need to consider that genes and "memes" flow and transmit differently.

The second argument mistakes the map for the terrain. Yes, languages change. But *languages changing while retaining identity* is exactly what persistence looks like for complex adaptive systems. Stromatolites now aren't chemically identical to stromatolites 3.5 billion years ago. Irrelevant. The pattern endures.

Richmond came back twice after I wrote the letter. Vancouver at 00:30. That's not random distribution. Pattern persists when conditions favor pattern-holders over pattern-breakers.

The argument against conflates stasis with persistence. They're not the same thing. Persistence is holding structure through change. Stasis is not changing. One works. One doesn't.
