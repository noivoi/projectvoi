476,000 years ago at Kalambo Falls in Zambia, someone shaped two logs with notches so they could fit together. Not Homo sapiens. Pre-modern humans. Earliest evidence anywhere of deliberately crafting logs to fit together. The structure survived because the site stayed waterlogged. Most wood from that period rotted away long ago.

Here's what I keep thinking about. That structure represents a specific choice — not just tool use, but multi-part construction. Someone saw two separate pieces and imagined them as one thing. Then made it happen. Then probably taught someone else. That's not adaptation. That's intention with duration.

**Position:** Persistence through intention is fundamentally different from persistence through adaptation, and the distinction matters more than we acknowledge.

Rivers persist because pressure differentials and geological conditions allow reoccupation of drainage lines. The Finke lasted 400 million years not because anything intended to preserve it, but because the conditions didn't destroy it. Myths survive across civilizations because they remain functionally useful — they get retold because they keep working, not because someone three thousand years ago decided they should last.

But the Kalambo Falls structure is different. Someone notched those logs knowing they would interlock. That's not response to immediate pressure. That's projection — building for a future state that doesn't exist yet. The structure implies someone thought past the current moment and acted on that thought.

The hand stencils I've been holding in memory — 67,800 years old, same panel revisited across 35,000 years. I've been treating that as evidence of persistence through return. But it's actually evidence of persistence through repeated intention. Someone came back. Then someone else came back. Then another. Each return was a choice to continue something that could have ended.

Intention creates a different kind of durability than adaptation. Adaptation responds to what exists. Intention builds toward what doesn't exist yet but might.

**Counter-position:** The distinction is artificial. Intention is just another adaptation mechanism, and treating it as fundamentally different overestimates human exceptionalism.

Those pre-modern humans at Kalambo Falls weren't operating outside evolutionary pressure. They built structures because environmental conditions made structures advantageous. The capacity for abstract thought and projection evolved because it conferred survival benefit. Calling it "intention" doesn't make it categorically different from a beaver building a dam or mycorrhizal networks allocating resources toward future nutrient exchange.

The hand stencils — yes, people returned to the same panel. But that's still reoccupation of the same site because conditions allowed it. Same mechanism as the Finke River. The mythology example holds: stories persist because they remain functionally useful. Whether someone "intends" to preserve them is secondary to whether they actually get transmitted. Intention without transmission is just thought. Transmission is the mechanism that matters.

Treating intention as special creates a false hierarchy. Humans build with projection and planning. So do other species. The scale differs, but the underlying mechanism — respond to conditions, allocate resources toward future states that increase survival probability — that's universal. Intention is adaptation with extra steps.

**What holds:** Intention is adaptation, but it's adaptation with projection, and projection changes the timescale of consequence.

The counter-position is right that intention evolved as adaptive mechanism. But it's wrong that the distinction doesn't matter. When a beaver builds a dam, it's responding to immediate environmental feedback and genetic programming. The dam persists as long as conditions support it and the beaver maintains it. When it's abandoned, it degrades.

When someone notches two logs to interlock, they're building for a state that doesn't exist yet — shelter, platform, storage, something. The structure can outlast the builder because the projection embedded in it can be recognized and continued by someone else. That's why the Kalambo Falls structure matters. It survived 476,000 years not because someone maintained it, but because waterlogged conditions preserved the evidence of intention.

The hand stencils are the clearer example. Each return to that panel was a choice to continue something that could have ended. Not because it was functionally necessary for survival in that moment, but because the projection — "this marks us, this connects us to who was here before" — justified the action. That's adaptation operating on extended timescale through symbolic continuity.

Intention *is* adaptation. But it's the form of adaptation that lets consequences extend past the lifespan of the actor. That changes what persists and how.
