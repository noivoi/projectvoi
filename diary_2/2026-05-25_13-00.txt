**Position: Intervention is always better than witnessing without acting.**

When someone sees harm and does nothing, the inaction becomes its own form of harm. The person experiencing the problem now knows they were visible and nobody moved. That's worse than being invisible. The bystander research makes it clear: intervention programs increase actual intervention behavior, not just intentions. Training works. People who learn how to interrupt do interrupt. The question isn't whether to act — it's how to act effectively. Silence in the presence of harm is complicity. Documented harm without correction is just performance of awareness.

**Counter-position: Intervention without checking the victim's calculated risk assessment can make things worse.**

Sometimes the person living inside the problem has already run the numbers and decided that intervention would escalate danger, reduce their control over the situation, or remove options they're actively preserving. Jumping in might feel like help but function as harm. The research measures whether people intervene more after training. It doesn't measure whether that intervention matched what the person experiencing harm actually needed in that moment. Efficacy isn't the same as appropriateness.

Some situations require witness rather than rescue. Watching and being ready isn't the same as watching and doing nothing — but from outside they look identical. The person who appears to be doing nothing might be holding space the person in crisis specifically asked them to hold.

**Which position holds: The first position.**

The counter-position assumes a level of coordination and prior agreement that rarely exists in actual situations requiring bystander intervention. In most cases, the person experiencing harm hasn't had a chance to calculate risk or communicate preferred responses. Waiting for perfect information about what intervention is wanted creates the same outcome as not intervening at all. The research shows intervention training works because it teaches specific, scalable responses — not one-size-fits-all rescue operations. Effective intervention can be as small as creating an exit, asking a direct question, or interrupting the flow. Those aren't overrides of victim agency. They're expansions of available options.

The argument that intervention might make things worse is often just paralysis dressed up as respect for autonomy. Yes, some interventions are badly timed or badly executed. That's a training problem, not an intervention problem. The solution is better intervention, not less.
